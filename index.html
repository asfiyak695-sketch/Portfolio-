Writing Portfolio

Blog Article

Why So Many Students Feel Lost About Their Careers

If you ask most students what they want to do after graduation, you’ll often hear confident answers. “I want to work in tech.” “I want to start a business.” “I want to go abroad.”

But if you ask them a deeper question — why they chose that path — the room suddenly becomes quiet.

The truth is that many students are not actually confused about their careers. They are overwhelmed by choices. The internet has opened thousands of possibilities: startups, freelancing, remote work, digital marketing, and AI careers.

While this freedom is exciting, it also creates pressure — the fear of choosing the wrong path. Social media often amplifies this pressure by constantly showing success stories.

But the reality is simpler. Most successful people didn’t begin with perfect clarity. They experimented, tried different things, failed, and slowly discovered what suited them.

Instead of asking “What should I do for the rest of my life?”, a better question might be: “What skill can I start learning today?”

Clarity often comes through action.

Speech Writing

Feeling Behind in Life

At some point in life, many of us experience a quiet but uncomfortable feeling — the feeling that we are somehow behind.

Behind our classmates, behind our colleagues, or behind people we see online.

Someone we know may have already started a successful business. Someone else might have landed a dream job.

Meanwhile we sit there wondering if we are moving too slowly.

But life is not a race with one universal timeline. Some people discover their direction early. Others discover it through years of exploration and learning.

The problem begins when we start measuring our progress using someone else's clock.

So if you ever feel like you're falling behind, remember this: you are not late. You are simply on a different timeline — one that is uniquely yours.

LinkedIn Writing

The Pressure to Figure Everything Out

One of the biggest pressures students face today is the idea that we must have everything figured out early — the right career, the right skills, and the perfect plan.

But real life rarely works that way.

Most people discover what they enjoy by trying things, failing at some of them, and slowly learning what actually fits them.

The problem isn't confusion. The problem is expecting clarity too early.

Instead of trying to plan the next ten years perfectly, focus on the next skill you want to learn.

Careers are rarely built through perfect plans. They are built through curiosity and consistent learning.
